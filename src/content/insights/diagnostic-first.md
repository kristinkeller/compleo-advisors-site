Most strategy engagements begin with a decision: what we are building. The brief gets written, the deliverables get scoped, and a senior team gets to work. The diagnostic gets compressed into a few stakeholder interviews and a few weeks of background reading, and then the real strategy work begins.

I have come to believe this is backwards.

The diagnostic is where the real work gets done. Without it, the strategy that follows is shaped more by the consultant's assumptions than by the organization's reality. The gap between what the strategy says and what the company can actually execute shows up later, expensively.

## What a diagnostic actually answers

A real diagnostic answers questions the brief does not.

What is the business actually trying to do, and what is in its way? Which stakeholders matter most over the next 18 months, and what do they already think they know? Where does the asset's commercial story sit in a field that is not standing still? Which internal capabilities are real, and which ones look real only on the org chart?

Those answers shape every choice that follows. Without them, the strategy is necessarily generic, because the strategist has no other choice.

## What skipping the diagnostic costs

In rare disease, the cost of generic strategy is unusually high. Small populations mean tight stakeholder networks. Advocacy communities read patterns across companies. Investigators talk. A positioning choice that would survive a quarter of B2B drift will not survive a year in rare disease without producing visible inconsistency.

I have seen launches arrive with messaging the field had already half-heard from a competitor. I have seen advocacy strategies that contradicted the medical narrative the same company was telling at congress. I have seen commercial plans built on assumptions about a payer landscape that had quietly shifted six months earlier. Every one of these would have been caught in a real diagnostic.

## What the diagnostic produces

A diagnostic does not produce a strategy. It produces the conditions under which a useful strategy can be built.

A clear point of view on what the organization is actually trying to accomplish. A mapped understanding of the stakeholders who matter and what they currently believe. A read of the field that is current, not borrowed from last year's deck. A sense of what the organization can credibly say, and what it can credibly do.

Strategy work that begins from here is faster, sharper, and far less likely to get rewritten after launch. The deliverables look similar to what they would have looked like otherwise. The difference is that they hold.

## Frame. Solve. Activate.

The diagnostic is the Frame step in how I work. It is the foundation that makes Solve and Activate specific to the organization, rather than generic to the category. Leading with Frame is not the fastest route to a deliverable, but it is the fastest route to a strategy that survives contact with the market.
